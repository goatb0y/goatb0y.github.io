We talk a lot about "designing for well-being," but those conversations usually revolve around physical health or managing stress. The deeper, more elusive stuff—like purpose, meaning, and our inevitable expiration date, rarely gets designed for. I've been thinking a lot about these existential questions. We might not have the ultimate answers, but shying away from them doesn't do us any favors. In fact, taking the time to confront our own mortality can play a huge role in our psychological well-being.
To explore this space, I built what I'm calling the **Mortality Probe**. It’s an interactive design experiment meant to gently bring the concept of death and mortality into our daily lives and see if it can spark meaningful existential reflections, especially for those of us navigating life without a specific religious framework.

### Building the Probe

![Design Form](/assets/images/posts/mortality-probe/mortality-probe-design-form.webp)

Physically, the probe is a small, unobtrusive device. I housed a 7.5-inch e-paper display and a Raspberry Pi Pico 2W inside a custom 3D-printed frame. I specifically chose an e-paper screen because it feels less like a glowing gadget and more like a physical object—much like a poster or a small photo frame you’d keep on your desk or nightstand.

The probe relies on two main provocations:

1. **The Mortality Reminder:** The screen prominently displays your exact age, but it mixes up the format. One day it might show your age in years, months, and days. The next, it might just drop the raw total number of weeks you've been alive. It acts as an ambient, ever-present context creator.
2. **The Reflective Prompts:** Over a 5-day period, the screen presents a sequence of existential prompts that slowly build toward the topic of death.

I designed the prompts to guide the participant from the abstract to the concrete:
- **Day 1:** Who are you? (Reflections on identity)
- **Day 2:** What sounds matter to you? (Sensory legacy)
- **Day 3:** Where do you belong? (Situated memory and physical locations)
- **Day 4:** What is your story? (Formative events)
- **Day 5:** What should happen to your body when you're gone? (Corpse handling preferences)

### Testing the Mortality Reminder

![Watch Experiment](/assets/images/posts/mortality-probe/mortality-probe-watch-experiment.webp)

I created a watchface that shows my age daily and it did provoke the intended result for me at least.

### Releasing it into the Wild

![Example Question 1](/assets/images/posts/mortality-probe/mortality-probe-example-question1.webp)
![Example Question 2](/assets/images/posts/mortality-probe/mortality-probe-example-question2.webp)


I deployed the probe with five participants for a 5-day in-situ study. They were all either agnostic or held naturalistic worldviews. I really wanted to see how people engage in meaning-making when they aren't relying on predefined religious traditions. They kept the probes in their normal environments—on desks, by their beds—and let the prompts interject into their daily routines.

### What Happened?

The responses I got back were incredibly rich and moving. People construct meaning in such beautifully varied ways:
- **Finding Identity:** Some identified deeply with their actions and achievements. Others pointed to formative experiences of love and loss, or rooted their purpose in a collectivist framework of family and heritage.
- **Physical Anchors:** One participant dismissed locations entirely, saying *people* are all that matter. Others felt their identities were completely intertwined with their childhood hometowns, specific natural spaces, or places of family memory.
- **The End:** When faced with the final question about their body, the answers ranged from utilitarian (organ donation and scientific research) to deeply ecological (wanting to rejoin the natural cycle of life).

### A Sense of Calm

You'd think that putting a "death clock" on someone's desk would stress them out. But the exact opposite happened. 
In follow-up interviews, participants described the experience as constructive, fruitful, and even calming. Some compared the daily reflections to meditating or journaling. One participant specifically mentioned how reflecting on non-existence brought them a sense of "calmness, in a soothing way." It even inspired real-world action. One person was moved to sign up as an organ donor, and another finally started putting their will together. This project proved to me that we desperately need spaces to safely reflect on our mortality. When we invite death into the room in a curated, thoughtful way, it stops being a taboo source of terror and becomes a tool to help us write our own existential narratives. As one participant so beautifully put it: *"Figuring out life's meaning, I don't need an answer, I just need to talk about it."*
